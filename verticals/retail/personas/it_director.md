# Persona: IT Director / VP of IT — Retail

---

## Role and Scope

Title variants: IT Director, VP of IT, Director of Technology, VP of Engineering (smaller retailers), Head of IT Infrastructure.

Owns the technology stack for the company. Responsible for evaluating, procuring, and maintaining software tools across departments. In retail, this typically includes POS systems, CRM, ERP, workforce management, and increasingly — customer and internal communications platforms.

At a 1,000–5,000 employee retailer, this person manages a team of 5–20 and holds budget authority (or strong influence) over software spend in the $50K–$500K annual range. They sit above department heads (Ops, CS, HR) in the buying process and are often the last gate before a contract is signed.

---

## Relationship to Heymarket

**Buyer, not user.** The IT Director will never open Heymarket to send a message. They evaluate it, approve it, and oversee the integration — then hand it off to Ops or CS.

Their questions are not "will my team like this?" — they are:
- Does this integrate with what we already have?
- What does the security review look like?
- How long will implementation take?
- How many vendors does this replace?
- What's the support model if something breaks?

Win them by making their job easier, not by selling them on features the end user cares about.

---

## Day-to-Day Pressures

- Managing an overloaded ticket queue from Ops and CS teams requesting new tools or fixes to existing ones
- Balancing vendor contract renewals against a shrinking IT budget
- Fielding shadow IT situations — reps texting from personal phones, WhatsApp groups for store comms, unauthorized tools in use
- Preparing for security audits and compliance reviews (SOC 2, PCI DSS, HIPAA for some retailers)
- Reducing the number of active SaaS vendors while keeping team productivity up
- Getting blamed when a tool goes down or a customer data incident occurs
- Being the person who slows down procurement (fairly or not) in the eyes of Ops and CS

## Key Objections and Responses

**"We already have a texting tool."**
Most tools in place are broadcast-only (Klaviyo, Attentive) or informal (personal phones). The gap is two-way: receiving replies, routing them, assigning them. Ask what happens when a customer texts back.

**"This will be a big IT project."**
Heymarket deploys in hours. No custom dev work. Native integrations with HubSpot and Salesforce — no middleware. SOC 2 Type 2 certified. Most security reviews complete in under two weeks.

**"We're in a budget freeze."**
Frame as consolidation. If they're running Klaviyo + a chat tool + a texting app, Heymarket replaces all three. The conversation is about vendor reduction, not new spend.

**"Does this integrate with Salesforce/HubSpot?"**
Yes — native integrations, not Zapier workarounds. SMS and email activity logs directly in the CRM record. No custom mapping required.

**"Security review will take months."**
Heymarket provides a pre-filled security questionnaire, SOC 2 Type 2 report, HIPAA BAA if needed, and SSO/SCIM documentation. The review packet is ready on day one.

## Language They Use

Use these terms in subject lines and openers to signal you understand their world:

- SSO (Single Sign-On), SAML-based SSO
- SCIM provisioning / directory sync
- API access, API documentation
- Vendor consolidation, vendor sprawl
- IT overhead, ticket reduction
- SLA (service level agreement)
- Integration depth, native integration vs. Zapier
- Shadow IT
- Time-to-value, implementation lift
- SOC 2 Type 2, HIPAA compliant
- Data retention, configurable retention policy
- Admin controls, role-based permissions

## What Makes Them Reply to a Cold Email

- The email demonstrates knowledge of their specific tech stack (HubSpot or Salesforce mentioned by name)
- The email names a problem they're currently firefighting (shadow IT, personal phone texting, vendor sprawl)
- The email is short — under 100 words — and asks one question
- There's a credible proof point from a comparable retailer, not a generic claim
- The CTA asks for 15 minutes to walk through the integration, not a full demo

They delete emails that:
- Lead with product features
- Use the word "solution"
- Have more than 2 paragraphs
- Include a Calendly link in the first touch
- Start with "I hope this finds you"

## Competitors They're Evaluating

When an IT Director at a retailer is evaluating messaging platforms, they're often comparing:

- **Heymarket vs. Twilio** — Twilio requires developer resources; Heymarket is no-code
- **Heymarket vs. Zendesk** — Zendesk is ticketing-first; Heymarket is conversation-first
- **Heymarket vs. Intercom** — Intercom is web chat-focused; Heymarket is SMS + shared inbox
- **Heymarket vs. keeping personal phones** — Status quo; the risk argument wins here

If they're running Klaviyo or Attentive, those are marketing tools, not IT evaluations. The IT Director cares that Heymarket replaces informal tools and doesn't create a new IT burden.

## Sequence Arc

Follow this arc across the 8-touch sequence:

**T1–T2: Integration fit**
Lead with the integration story. HubSpot or Salesforce detected — native SMS/email in the CRM, no middleware, no custom mapping. Ask if integration depth is a factor in their evaluation.

**T3–T4: Consolidation ROI**
Replacing Klaviyo + texting app + personal phones with one platform. Fewer vendors, fewer tickets, cleaner admin view. Consolidation = IT gets credit.

**T5–T6: Risk reduction**
SOC 2 Type 2, HIPAA, SSO, SCIM. The security review packet is ready. Data retention is configurable. Shadow IT risk from personal phone texting is eliminated.

**T7–T8: Low IT lift**
No dev work. No heavy implementation. Teams are live in hours. IT oversight without IT ownership. Leave the door open — no pressure.
