# Persona: Retail Ops Manager / Head of CS / Store Ops

---

## Role and Scope

Title variants: Retail Operations Manager, Head of Customer Service, Director of Store Operations, VP of Operations, Customer Experience Manager, Store Ops Lead.

Day-to-day owner of how the retail team communicates with customers and store associates. Responsible for response times, CSAT scores, shift coverage, issue resolution, and team productivity. In a 1,000–5,000 employee retailer, this person manages 10–100 front-line staff across multiple locations or channels.

They don't set IT strategy — but they feel every failure of the tools IT chooses. They are the loudest voice in the room when something isn't working, and the strongest champion when something solves their problem.

---

## Relationship to Heymarket

**Primary user and internal champion.** The Ops Manager or Head of CS is the person whose team will use Heymarket every day. They feel the pain before the purchase and drive adoption after it.

They are not the final signer — IT Director or CFO often holds budget — but they are the reason the deal moves. A cold email that earns a reply from this persona turns into an IT evaluation meeting within two weeks if the pain is real.

Their questions:
- Will my team actually use this?
- How fast can we get set up?
- What does the shared inbox look like?
- Can I see all my team's messages in one place?
- What happens when a customer texts back?

---

## Day-to-Day Pressures

- Watching customer texts go unanswered because they landed on one person's phone and that person is off shift
- Managing a team that texts customers from personal numbers — no record, no oversight, no handoff when someone quits
- Trying to reach store associates quickly for shift changes or coverage gaps with no reliable channel
- Fielding CSAT complaints that trace back to slow response times or dropped conversations
- Dealing with informal WhatsApp groups for team comms — functional but invisible to management
- Justifying headcount by showing output, but having no data because conversations happen in personal inboxes
- Rolling out new tools that get used for two weeks and then abandoned

## Key Objections and Responses

**"My team won't adopt another tool."**
Heymarket is SMS — your team already knows how to text. The interface is simpler than email. Adoption at comparable retailers is tracked at 80%+ within 30 days because there's no learning curve.

**"We already have a system for this."**
What happens when a customer replies? Most systems (Klaviyo, Attentive, email blasts) can't receive replies. If a customer texts back and nobody sees it, the system isn't working.

**"We use WhatsApp for team comms."**
That's a compliance and visibility risk. When an associate leaves, the conversation history goes with their personal account. Heymarket gives you the same speed with full admin visibility and opt-out compliance.

**"We're too busy to implement anything right now."**
Setup takes a few hours. You use your existing phone number. No IT project. Your team sends their first message the same day.

**"I don't have budget authority."**
That's fine. If this solves the response time problem, you're the person who makes the case to IT. We can walk you through what that conversation looks like.

## What Makes Them Reply to a Cold Email

- The email names a specific pain they dealt with this week (missed messages, personal phone texting, slow response times)
- There's a peer proof point — another retailer, a specific outcome, a number they can visualize
- The email is short and asks one question that's easy to answer
- The CTA is low commitment — "does this sound familiar?" not "book a 30-minute demo"
- It doesn't sound like a vendor pitch — it sounds like someone who's talked to people like them before

They delete emails that:
- Lead with platform features
- Ask for a 30-minute call in the first sentence
- Use the word "solution" or "leverage"
- Have three paragraphs before making a point
- Sound like they were written for IT, not operations

## Best Proof Points for This Persona

- **U-Haul — 60% response rate** using enterprise SMS for dispatch and operations. Same context: team coordination, customer communication, high volume.
- **Healthcare vertical — 25% response rate**: "You're more likely to get a response via text." Applies to any customer-facing ops team.
- **Shared inbox** — any team member can read, reply, or assign messages. No more conversations dying on one person's phone.
- **Automations** — auto-assign by keyword, auto-reply outside business hours, auto-enroll in follow-up sequence. Ops gets leverage without adding headcount.
- **CSAT improvement** — faster response times via shared inbox directly improve customer satisfaction scores, which is how this persona is measured.
- **Deployment speed** — teams are live in hours. No IT project. No training program. If the team can text, they can use Heymarket.

## Sequence Arc

Follow this arc across the 8-touch sequence:

**T1–T2: Daily pain**
Start with the specific operational pain they're living with today — missed messages, personal phone texting, or dropped customer conversations. Make them feel seen before selling anything.

**T3–T4: Quick win proof**
Introduce a specific outcome from a similar company. U-Haul 60% response rate. Healthcare 25% response rate. A number they can bring to their boss to justify the conversation.

**T5–T6: Peer story**
Go deeper on the peer proof. Name the company, name the problem they had, name the outcome. If HubSpot or Salesforce is detected, introduce the integration angle at T6 — "your CRM team sees every conversation."

**T7–T8: Low pressure**
Break-up touch is gentle. No urgency. "If response time isn't the right problem right now, no worries." Re-engage at T8 with a fresh angle — new proof point or new pain angle — no reference to prior silence.
