# Program Rules — Heymarket Outreach Optimizer

---

## SEQUENCE STRUCTURE

8 touches per sequence. Follow day cadence exactly.

| Touch | Day  | Type                  | Purpose                                               |
|-------|------|-----------------------|-------------------------------------------------------|
| T1    | 1    | Cold intro            | Hook on specific pain, single reply CTA               |
| T2    | 4    | Follow-up             | Different angle, one proof point                      |
| T3    | 9    | Value add             | Customer story or stat                                |
| T4    | 13   | Soft bump             | 2–3 lines, "did this land?"                           |
| T5    | 18   | New angle             | Different use case or pain                            |
| T6    | 22   | Integration angle     | HubSpot or Salesforce tie-in                          |
| T7    | 29   | Break-up              | Low pressure, leave door open                         |
| T8    | 59   | Re-engage             | Fresh opener, no reference to prior silence           |

---

## SCORING RUBRIC

Score each touch 1–10 on each criterion. Max score per touch: 60.

1. **Hook strength** — Does line 1 earn a read in 5 seconds? No throat-clearing, no "I hope this finds you."
2. **Persona relevance** — Does this speak to the specific title's actual daily pain, not a generic pitch?
3. **Proof** — Is there a real customer name, stat, or specific integration mentioned? Generic claims score 1–3.
4. **CTA quality** — Single ask, reply-only, zero friction. No "book a demo," no links, no calendly.
5. **Brevity** — Under 150 words. No filler phrases. No bullets. No bold. Tighter = higher score.
6. **Human tone** — Sounds like a person wrote it in 10 minutes. Not a template. Not marketing copy.

## SEQUENCE ARC RULES

Arcs vary by buyer type. Follow the arc order when selecting pain angles across T1–T8.

**IT Buyer arc:**
integration fit → consolidation ROI → risk reduction → low IT lift

- T1–T2: Lead with integration fit (HubSpot/Salesforce depth, API, SSO)
- T3–T4: Consolidation ROI — ticket reduction, tool sprawl, vendor count
- T5–T6: Risk reduction — compliance, HIPAA if relevant, security review speed
- T7–T8: Low IT lift — fast deployment, no heavy implementation, time-to-value

**Ops Buyer arc:**
daily pain → quick win proof → peer story → low pressure

- T1–T2: Lead with the daily operational pain they feel today
- T3–T4: Quick win proof — specific stat or outcome from a similar company
- T5–T6: Peer story — another retail brand, same problem, solved with Heymarket
- T7–T8: Low pressure — no urgency, just a door left open

## OUTREACH FORMAT RULES

Apply to every touch in every sequence.

- **Subject lines**: lowercase only, under 8 words, no punctuation, no emoji
- **Body**: plain text only — no bullets, no bold, no HTML, no markdown
- **Tokens**: only use {{first_name}}, {{company}}, {{title}} — nothing else
- **Signature**: end every touch with exactly: [SIGNATURE]
- **Rep task note**: include one line of context for the rep after [SIGNATURE], prefixed with "Rep note:"
- **Length**: hard limit 150 words per touch body. Variants over 150 words are disqualified.
- **Forbidden phrases**: "hope this finds you", "I wanted to reach out", "touching base", "circling back", "quick question", "synergy", "solution", "leverage", "at scale" (unless in a proof point)
- **CTA rule**: one ask per touch, reply-based only. No links. No calendar invites. No "book a demo."

## CORE COMPETITIVE WEDGE

Embed this structural difference in sequences — especially T1, T5, and T7.

**Heymarket = two-way conversational messaging**
**Competitors (Klaviyo, Braze, Attentive, Brevo) = one-way broadcast**

They send. Heymarket replies.

- Competitors push messages out. Heymarket creates a channel where customers can respond, be routed, be assigned, and be served by a real person.
- Broadcast tools optimize for send volume. Heymarket optimizes for conversation quality and response rates.
- When a customer replies to a Klaviyo campaign, nobody sees it. When a customer replies to a Heymarket message, it routes to the right team member's shared inbox.
- Use this wedge when the prospect is already paying for a broadcast tool — not to attack the tool, but to identify the gap.

## SEARCH SPACE — IT Director (retail)

Use these 8 pain angles to generate T1 variants. Rotate through angles across iterations. No angle repeated until all 8 have been used.

Pain angle 1: **Vendor sprawl** — Too many messaging tools with no consolidation. IT is managing contracts for Klaviyo, Attentive, a texting app, and a chat widget separately. No unified admin view.

Pain angle 2: **IT overhead** — Reps texting from personal phones. No visibility, no audit trail, no way to offboard a rep without losing conversation history. IT gets the support tickets.

Pain angle 3: **Integration risk** — "Will this work with our HubSpot/Salesforce?" is the first question from procurement. Deep native integrations reduce IT review cycles and implementation risk.

Pain angle 4: **Security review burden** — SSO, SCIM provisioning, API key management, data retention policies. Every new tool adds 3–6 weeks of security review. Heymarket is SOC 2 Type 2, HIPAA-compliant, and passes most reviews in under two weeks.

Pain angle 5: **Tool consolidation ROI** — Replacing 2–3 point solutions with one platform reduces ticket volume, SLA overhead, and vendor management time. IT gets credit for the consolidation.

Pain angle 6: **Low IT lift** — No heavy implementation. No custom dev work. Teams are live in hours, not weeks. IT doesn't have to own the rollout.

Pain angle 7: **Competitive displacement** — Already paying for Klaviyo or Attentive. Getting broadcast-only capabilities at enterprise pricing. Heymarket adds the two-way layer they're missing.

Pain angle 8: **Two-way gap** — Current stack can only send. Can't receive replies. Customer responses go nowhere. IT has fielded requests from Ops/CS teams to solve this and hasn't found a clean answer yet.

## SEARCH SPACE — Ops Manager (retail)

Use these 8 pain angles to generate T1 variants. Rotate through angles across iterations. No angle repeated until all 8 have been used.

Pain angle 1: **Personal phone texting** — Team uses personal numbers to text customers and store associates. No record. When someone leaves, the conversation history goes with them. Compliance and visibility risk.

Pain angle 2: **Missed messages** — No shared inbox means customer texts land on one person's phone. When that person is off shift or out sick, messages go unanswered for hours. CSAT drops.

Pain angle 3: **Shift coordination** — No fast, reliable way to reach store associates across a shift. Manager texts from personal phone, associates miss messages, coverage gaps happen.

Pain angle 4: **Slow response times** — Customers expect a reply in minutes, not hours. Without a shared inbox and routing rules, response times are inconsistent and CSAT suffers.

Pain angle 5: **Tool adoption fear** — Team won't learn another platform. Last rollout had 30% adoption after 60 days. Ops needs something associates actually use.

Pain angle 6: **WhatsApp already in use** — Informal WhatsApp groups are already running for team comms. No admin visibility, no opt-out management, no compliance. IT is aware and uncomfortable.

Pain angle 7: **Peer proof** — Another retail brand the prospect respects solved this exact problem with Heymarket. U-Haul: 60% response rate using enterprise SMS. Specific, credible, same industry.

Pain angle 8: **Two-way gap** — Current tools (Klaviyo, Attentive, email blasts) broadcast to customers but can't receive replies. When a customer responds, nobody sees it. Conversations fall through.
